# Chapter 12: What's Next?

The awards ceremony happened right there on the dock, with everyone still dripping.

Ron held up the microphone. "First place and the Golden Paddle, for being the first team in Millbrook Cardboard Regatta history to complete the course: Aanya and Priya Patel, in the Stiletto!"

Aanya and Priya held up the Golden Paddle together. It was an actual paddle, spray-painted gold, and it was the most beautiful ugly thing Remi had ever seen.

"Second place, with a time of twelve minutes and forty-seven seconds: Remi Park, in the Narwhal!"

Remi walked up and took her trophy, which was a small wooden plaque with a cardboard boat glued to it. She held it up and the crowd cheered, and she felt her face get hot and her smile get so wide it practically wrapped around her head.

"Best Sinking Award, for the most spectacular failure: the Kozlov twins, in the Red Menace!"

Mila and Sasha bowed like they'd won an Oscar. They got a trophy that was a tiny cardboard boat glued to a rock, which was perfect.

"And a special mention: Marcus Chen, for setting a new personal best of eight minutes and twelve seconds in his fourth year of competition."

Marcus gave a salute. He looked happy. Seven minutes fourteen to eight minutes twelve. A whole minute better.

After the ceremony, something unexpected happened.

The Patel sisters walked over to Remi.

Aanya was carrying the Golden Paddle and Priya was carrying her tablet, and they both looked at Remi like they were studying a rare animal.

"Your boat," Aanya said. "Can we look at it?"

"Sure," Remi said.

They walked down to where the Narwhal was still floating at the dock, tied off with a piece of rope. Aanya crouched down and inspected the hull. She pressed the bottom with her palm. She looked at the seams. She peered at the triangular cutouts in the sides.

"Your waterproofing is incredible," Aanya said. "We had our boat sealed with four layers of tape and we STILL got a small leak in the last two minutes. But your hull is completely dry."

"Waxed cardboard base, plus duct tape with overlap seams, plus latex paint on the seam lines," Remi said.

"Wax. That's smart. Where did you get waxed cardboard?"

"Produce boxes from the grocery store. They're coated on the inside to hold wet fruits and vegetables."

Priya tapped something on her tablet. "Waxed produce boxes," she murmured. "Good data."

Aanya stood up. "Your boat is slower than ours because it's wider and heavier. But it lasted longer in the water. If you made it narrower and took out more weight..."

"I'd have to sacrifice some stability," Remi said. "Narrower means tippier."

"Unless you add a skeg."

"A what?"

"A skeg. Like a small fin on the bottom of the hull. It keeps the boat tracking straight AND adds stability, like a keel. We have one." Aanya pointed at the underside of the Stiletto, where a small ridge of folded cardboard ran along the center line of the hull.

Remi's brain did the thing. The spinning thing. The sorting-through-bins thing.

"A skeg," she repeated, already seeing it in her head. A fold of cardboard along the Narwhal's bottom, maybe an inch deep, running from the bow to the stern. It would cut resistance AND add stability AND she could make it from a single strip of the waxed cardboard she already had.

"Hey," Aanya said. She stuck out her hand. "Good race."

Remi shook it. "Good race. Your boat is amazing."

"Yours is too. Ours is just faster." Aanya grinned. "For now."

Marcus came over next. He was still damp and his boat was gone, dissolved into a pile of soggy cardboard that he'd fished out of the pond and stuffed into a trash bag.

"OK," he said. "You HAVE to tell me about the waterproofing."

Remi told him. She told him about the twelve test squares, about the wax and the paint and the shingle-style seams. She pulled out her Build Journal and showed him the experiment data. Marcus's eyes kept getting wider.

"You did controlled experiments," he said. "With a control group and everything."

"Of course."

"I've just been... guessing. For three years."

"Stop guessing," Remi said. "Start testing."

Marcus laughed. "Can I take a picture of your data?"

"I'll do you one better. Come by the Garage of Doom next week. I'll show you the whole process."

"The Garage of Doom." Marcus shook his head. "That's what your workshop is called?"

"That's what it's NAMED. There's a sign and everything."

"Of course there is."

On the drive home, Remi sat in the back seat with the trophy in her lap and the Narwhal Mark Three strapped to the roof of Dad's car. Lucas was asleep next to her, still wearing his life vest (he'd refused to take it off). Noor was in the front seat, transcribing her race notes into a clean copy.

Mom turned around from the passenger seat. "I'm proud of you, Remi."

"Thanks, Mom."

"The boat was really something."

"It was cardboard and tape."

"It was cardboard and tape and two weeks of work and about fifty hours of your time and a whole lot of math and science and stubbornness. That's really something." Mom paused. "Your halmeoni would have loved that boat."

Remi looked out the window. The town was sliding by, houses and trees and mailboxes, all ordinary, all the same as they'd been two weeks ago when she'd first seen the flyer. But something felt different. She felt different.

She'd had a problem. She'd researched it. She'd designed a solution. She'd built it. It had failed. She'd figured out why. She'd rebuilt it. It had failed differently. She'd figured out why again. She'd rebuilt it again. And then it had worked.

That was the whole thing. That was the secret, if there even was one. It wasn't about being smart or having good materials or being lucky. It was about not stopping when it didn't work the first time.

Or the second time.

Or the third time.

"Noor," Remi said.

"Yeah?"

"Mr. Henderson said there's a state-level cardboard boat race. Teams of three."

Noor turned around. She had the look on her face. The look that said she already knew what Remi was about to say.

"The Patel sisters are a team of two," Remi said.

"You want to ask them to join up?"

"I want to ask Aanya about that skeg. And Priya's data collection is better than ours. And their boat is faster. And our boat is more waterproof. And together..."

"Together you'd build something that nobody could beat."

"Maybe."

"Probably."

"OK, probably."

Noor grinned. "I'll text Priya. She gave me her number after the race."

"Of course she did."

When they got home, Remi carried the Narwhal Mark Three back into the Garage of Doom and set it on the sawhorses. She stood there for a minute, looking at it. This weird, wonderful, silver-and-gray cardboard boat that had started as a flyer on a workbench and ended as the second boat in Millbrook history to cross Miller's Pond.

She opened her Build Journal to a fresh page.

At the top, she wrote:

**PROJECT NARWHAL: STATE CHAMPIONSHIPS**

Then she paused.

She didn't know what the state race would be like. She didn't know how far the course was, or what kind of water, or what the other teams would build. She didn't know if the Patel sisters would even say yes.

She didn't know any of that.

She picked up her pencil and started drawing anyway.

That was the thing about building. You never knew exactly how it was going to turn out. You just started, and you figured it out as you went, and when something broke, you fixed it, and when something didn't work, you tried something else, and you kept going until you ran out of tape or time or both.

Remi had plenty of tape.

And she wasn't anywhere close to running out of time.
