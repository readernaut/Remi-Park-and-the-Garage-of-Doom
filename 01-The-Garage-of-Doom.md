# Chapter 1: The Garage of Doom

Some kids had bedrooms. Remi Park had a bedroom AND a workshop, and if she was being totally honest, the workshop won by a mile.

It used to be half the garage. Then it was three-quarters of the garage. Now it was basically the entire garage, and Dad's car lived in the driveway full-time, which Dad said was "fine" and Mom said was "temporary," but they'd been saying both those things for a year and a half.

The neighborhood kids called it the Garage of Doom.

This was because of the noises. Buzzing, hammering, the occasional small bang, and once a sound that Mr. Henderson next door described as "like a robot screaming." (That was the fan motor. It was fine.)

Remi thought the Garage of Doom was an excellent name. She'd painted it over the door in red letters. Mom said "hmm."

Inside, the Garage of Doom had a wooden workbench that Dad built for Remi's seventh birthday. Mom was the one who taught Remi how to use the tools on it. She'd learned from her own mother, Halmeoni, who fixed everything in her house and thought calling a repairman was for people who gave up too easily. It had pegboard walls with hooks for every tool, and every tool had its outline traced in black marker so you always knew where it went back. It had a shelf of bins labeled in Remi's handwriting: GEARS, SPRINGS, WIRE, MAGNETS, THINGS THAT SPIN, and the biggest bin of all, STUFF THAT MIGHT BE USEFUL SOMEDAY.

That last bin was overflowing. It always was.

Right now, at 8:47 on a Saturday morning, Remi was testing the Marshmallow Mark Six.

The Marshmallow Mark Six was a catapult. It was made from a wooden spoon, three rubber bands, a toilet paper tube, and a piece of plywood. Versions one through five were in the recycling bin, except for version three, which was stuck in the big oak tree out back, and version five, which had caught fire. (A small fire. Barely worth mentioning.)

Remi loaded a marshmallow. She pulled back the spoon. She aimed at the oak tree.

She let go.

The marshmallow sailed up, up, up... and smacked into the kitchen window.

"REMI!" Mom yelled from inside.

"Sorry! Calibrating!"

Calibrating meant adjusting something until it worked exactly right. It sounded a lot more professional than "whoops, too far."

Remi adjusted the rubber band tension by half a turn, loaded another marshmallow, and fired.

*Thwack.* The marshmallow arced through the morning air, cleared the back fence by three feet, and disappeared into Mr. Henderson's yard.

"YES!" Remi punched the air. Then she heard Mr. Henderson say, "Why is there a marshmallow in my coffee?"

Remi decided it was time for breakfast.

She ran inside, where her little brother Lucas was already at the table, eating cereal and reading the back of the cereal box like it contained the secrets of the universe.

Lucas was six. He read everything. Street signs. Shampoo bottles. The little paper instructions inside Band-Aid boxes. Last week he'd read the entire Terms and Conditions for a website and announced, "They can use your data for marketing purposes, Remi."

"Hey," Remi said, sliding into her chair.

"Hey," said Lucas. "You launched another marshmallow at Mr. Henderson."

"Into his YARD. Not at HIM. Important difference."

"Is it, though?"

Mom set plates of garlic fried rice and eggs in front of both of them. "New rule. No projectiles before 9 AM."

"What about after 9 AM?"

Mom pointed the spatula at her. "Don't push it."

Dad came in from the hallway, holding his phone. "Morning, everybody. Remi, I moved my car again. Your... device... was blocking the driver's side door."

"That's the Marshmallow Mark Six. I'll move it after breakfast."

"The Marshmallow Mark Six," Dad repeated. "Better than the Mark Five?"

"The Mark Five caught fire."

"Right. The fire."

"It was a SMALL fire."

Mom sighed. But it was a sigh with a smile hiding underneath, and Remi caught it.

After breakfast, Remi went back to the Garage of Doom. She had the whole Saturday ahead of her and a new project to start: a machine that could fold paper airplanes automatically, six per minute, each one perfectly creased. She flipped her Build Journal open to a fresh page and started sketching.

Her Build Journal was a thick spiral notebook with a duct-tape cover she'd decorated herself. On the front, in silver paint pen, it said REMI PARK, ENGINEER. She was currently on page 247. Every single page was filled with sketches, measurements, arrows, notes, crossed-out designs, and little stars next to the ideas that actually worked.

She was deep into the paper-airplane-folding design when the garage door rattled and Noor's face appeared.

Noor Aziz was Remi's best friend and the second-most important person in the Garage of Doom. Remi was the one who designed things and built things and occasionally set things on fire. Noor was the one who kept lists, timed tests, recorded results, and said things like "Remi, that's going to explode" right before it exploded.

Every great engineer needed a Noor.

"Remi," Noor said. She was out of breath, like she'd run the whole way from her house, which was three blocks away. She was holding a piece of paper.

"What?"

"You need to see this." Noor slapped the paper onto the workbench.

It was a flyer. Bright blue, with a picture of a cardboard boat on it.

Remi read it.

Her eyes went wide.

She read it again.

Her eyes went wider.

"Noor," she said.

"I know."

"A cardboard boat race."

"I KNOW."

"Across Miller's Pond."

"I KNOW!"

Remi looked up from the flyer. Her brain was already doing the thing it did, the thing where a problem showed up and her mind started spinning like a wheel, clicking through bins, sorting through possibilities, building something in her imagination before her hands even knew what was happening.

"We're doing this," Remi said.

Noor grinned. "Obviously."
