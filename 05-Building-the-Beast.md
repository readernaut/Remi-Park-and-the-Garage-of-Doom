# Chapter 5: Building the Beast

Building a boat out of cardboard is harder than it sounds.

And it already sounds pretty hard.

The first thing Remi learned was that cutting cardboard is LOUD. The box cutter made a satisfying *schhhk* sound with every slice, and since the hull required approximately one million cuts (Noor's estimate was four hundred and twelve, but Remi's felt more accurate), the Garage of Doom sounded like a giant zipper being opened and closed over and over for three straight hours.

Mom came out at one point, looked at the carnage of cardboard scraps covering every surface, and said, "I'm going to pretend I didn't see this."

"Wise," said Noor.

Remi started with the bottom of the hull: the flat piece that would actually sit in the water. This was the most important part. If the bottom failed, nothing else mattered.

She cut a piece of double-wall waxed cardboard seven feet long and three feet wide. It was beautiful. She laid it on the garage floor and stared at it like an artist staring at a blank canvas.

"OK, now the second layer."

"Second layer?" Lucas looked up from his tape-cutting station. He had a pile of precisely eight-inch duct tape strips, each one carefully peeled and stuck to the edge of the workbench.

"The bottom gets THREE layers," Remi said. "Each one oriented in a different direction."

She held up two pieces of cardboard. "See these little lines running through the cardboard? That's the flute, the wavy part inside. It runs in one direction, and the cardboard is strongest going ACROSS the flutes. If you bend it along the flutes, it folds easy. If you try to bend it across them, it fights you."

She demonstrated, bending each piece. Lucas's eyes went wide.

"So if I stack three layers, and each one has the flutes running in a different direction..." She rotated the second piece 45 degrees from the first. "Now the bottom is strong in EVERY direction. It's like plywood. That's actually how plywood works, layers of wood with the grain going different ways."

"Cross-lamination!" Lucas shouted.

Remi and Noor both turned and stared at him.

"I read about it," Lucas said. "On the back of a plywood company's website."

"Of course you did," Remi said. She held up her hand for a high five. Lucas smacked it.

They glued the three layers of the hull bottom together with a thin layer of wood glue that Dad donated from his barely-used tool kit. ("I'm never getting my garage back, am I?" "No, Dad. No, you're not.")

Wait, could they use glue? Remi checked the rules. Cardboard, duct tape, and paint. Hmm. She looked at the glue.

"We can't use glue," she said.

"WHAT?" Noor looked at the freshly glued triple-layer bottom.

"The rules say cardboard, duct tape, and paint. Nothing about glue."

They stared at the hull bottom. They'd used a whole bottle of wood glue.

Remi set her jaw. "We're starting over."

"Remi, we spent two hours on that!"

"I know. But if we break the rules, we get disqualified. The bottom has to be held together with duct tape only."

Noor looked like she wanted to argue. But she didn't. Because she knew Remi was right, and Noor always respected the rules, even when the rules were annoying.

They peeled the layers apart (this was messy and horrible and involved a lot of sticky cardboard chunks stuck to everything) and started over.

This time, Remi laid the first layer flat, covered it completely in overlapping strips of duct tape (sticky side up), then pressed the second layer on top. Tape between every layer. Tape on the outside. Tape on the seams. Tape on the tape.

"That's a lot of tape," Lucas said again.

"It's structural," Remi said. And it was, because the duct tape wasn't just sealing the layers, it was holding them together. Each strip of tape became a tiny bridge connecting the layers, making the whole sandwich stronger than any single layer could be.

When the three-layer bottom was done, Remi knocked on it with her knuckles. It sounded like knocking on a door.

"Solid," she said. "Now the ribs."

The ribs were Remi's favorite part.

She cut ten pieces of B-flute cardboard into curves that matched the shape of the hull: flat on the bottom, curving up on the sides, like an upside-down U. Each rib was about six inches tall at the sides and three feet wide.

But here's the thing she'd learned at the library that changed everything: she folded each rib into a triangle shape.

"Lucas," she said. "Come here. I need to show you something."

She made a square out of four popsicle sticks and tape. She set it on the table.

"Push the top," she said.

Lucas pushed. The square collapsed into a diamond.

"Now watch." She made a triangle out of three popsicle sticks and tape.

"Push the top."

Lucas pushed. The triangle didn't budge. He pushed harder. Nothing. He leaned on it with both hands.

It held.

"TRIANGLES!" Lucas yelled.

"Triangles are the strongest shape," Remi said. "A square has too many ways it can fold. But a triangle locks every side in place. Push one side and the other two push back. That's why bridges and towers use triangles everywhere."

"So the ribs will be triangles?"

"The ribs will be U-shapes with triangular supports. Like this." She sketched it in her Build Journal: a rib that curved up the sides of the hull, with little triangular gussets at the base where the rib met the floor. The gussets would keep the ribs from folding sideways.

One by one, she cut and shaped ten ribs. Noor taped the triangular gussets onto each one. Lucas handed them tape strips, exactly eight inches, one after another, like a factory line.

They attached the ribs to the hull bottom, spaced ten inches apart, running from one side to the other. Each rib was taped at the base, at the sides, and at the top. When all ten ribs were in place, the hull bottom looked like the skeleton of a whale.

"Now the skin," Remi said.

The skin was the outer walls of the boat: the sides and the bow. Remi cut long panels of double-wall cardboard and bent them up to meet the ribs. The pointed bow was the trickiest part. She had to cut a V-shaped piece and fold it carefully so it came to a clean point, then tape it to the first rib on each side.

She messed this up twice. The first time, the cardboard cracked at the fold. The second time, the two halves of the V didn't meet evenly and the bow looked like a dented nose.

On the third try, she scored the fold line first, dragging the box cutter lightly along the surface without cutting all the way through. This let the cardboard bend smoothly without cracking.

"Score lines!" she said, holding up the perfect fold. "You can control where cardboard bends by scoring it first!"

"I'm writing that down," Noor said, and she did.

By the end of the day, the Garage of Doom looked like a disaster zone. Cardboard scraps covered the floor like weird snow. Duct tape stuck to everything, including the cat, who had wandered in at some point and was now wearing a small silver cape.

But in the middle of it all, sitting on two sawhorses, was a thing that looked like a boat.

It was seven feet long, three feet wide, and fourteen inches deep. It had a pointed bow and a flat stern. It had ten internal ribs and a triple-layer bottom. It was covered in silver duct tape and it gleamed under the garage lights.

The Narwhal.

"It's beautiful," Noor said.

"It's a boat," Lucas said, like he was surprised.

"It's not a boat yet," Remi said. She ran her hand along the hull, feeling every seam, every edge, every place where two pieces of tape overlapped. "It's a shape. A boat is a shape that floats. We don't know if it floats yet."

She looked at her team.

"Water test. Saturday."

Noor nodded.

Lucas saluted.

The cat, still wearing its duct-tape cape, meowed.

Saturday was going to be very interesting.
