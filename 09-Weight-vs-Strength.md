# Chapter 9: Weight vs. Strength

This was the hardest problem Remi had ever worked on.

Not the hardest BUILDING problem. The hardest THINKING problem. Because it wasn't about adding something or fixing something. It was about taking things away without breaking what was already working.

She sat in the Garage of Doom with the Narwhal in front of her and her Build Journal open to a blank page, and she thought about it for a long time.

The boat weighed 47 pounds. She needed it closer to 30. That meant removing 17 pounds of material. But every piece of material was there for a reason: the three-layer bottom kept it waterproof, the ribs kept it from collapsing, the sides kept the water out.

If she removed the wrong thing, the boat would either leak, crumple, or sink.

"I need to figure out where the weight IS," she said to Noor, who was sitting on an overturned bucket, eating an apple.

"How?"

"By weighing every part separately."

They spent an afternoon taking the Narwhal partially apart and weighing each component on the kitchen scale:

- Triple-layer hull bottom: 19 pounds
- Ten internal ribs: 8 pounds
- Side panels (both sides): 12 pounds
- Bow section: 4 pounds
- Duct tape (total): estimated 4 pounds

"The bottom is almost half the total weight," Remi said, staring at the numbers.

"Makes sense," Noor said. "Three layers of double-wall cardboard plus tape on both sides plus paint. It's basically a sandwich of a sandwich."

"But that's what makes it waterproof. If I go to two layers, it might not hold up for fifteen minutes."

"What about the sides? Twelve pounds seems like a lot."

Remi picked up a side panel and looked at it. It was double-wall cardboard, taped inside and out. Solid and strong. But...

"The sides aren't in the water," she said slowly.

"What?"

"The sides don't sit in the water. The BOTTOM is submerged. The sides just need to keep splashes out. They don't need to be waterproof, they just need to be tall enough and stiff enough."

She held the panel up to the light. "What if I cut holes in the sides?"

"Won't the water come in through the holes?"

"Not if the holes are above the waterline. And the holes make the sides lighter without making them weaker, as long as I do it right."

She grabbed a piece of scrap cardboard and cut out a triangle. Then she cut a circle. Then another triangle.

"Think of it like a bridge," she said. "A solid bridge is heavy. But a bridge with triangle-shaped cutouts, a truss, is almost as strong and WAY lighter. The triangles keep the structure rigid while removing material from the places where it isn't doing any work."

"Like Swiss cheese?" Lucas said. He'd wandered in and was watching with great interest.

"Like... structural Swiss cheese. Yes."

"I like cheese."

"That's... not the point, but OK."

Remi sketched a new side-panel design in her Build Journal. Instead of solid walls of double-wall cardboard, each side panel would be single-wall cardboard with large triangular cutouts, each triangle reinforced with a duct-tape border. The tape around the edge of each cutout would keep the cardboard from tearing. The triangular shapes would keep the panel rigid.

She grabbed a side panel and her box cutter and started cutting. The first triangle came out clean. The second one too. She was getting confident. She cut a third, a fourth, a fifth...

The side panel folded in half.

"Oh no," Noor said.

Remi held up the panel. It flopped over like a wet towel. She'd cut too many triangles too close together, and the cardboard between them was so narrow it couldn't hold itself up.

"Too many holes," Remi said.

"You turned it into a paper snowflake," Lucas said.

She grabbed a fresh piece of cardboard and started again. This time she planned the cutouts on paper first, spacing them wider, making sure the strips of cardboard between each triangle were at least three inches wide. She tested the panel by holding it up and pressing on it. It flexed slightly but held.

"Lesson learned," she said. "You can't just cut holes everywhere and hope for the best. You have to leave enough material to carry the load between the holes."

"Should I write that down?" Noor asked.

"Definitely write that down."

The new side panels, properly cut, were stiff and light. When Remi knocked on one, it made a hollow, drum-like sound.

"This should cut the side-panel weight in half," Remi said. "From twelve pounds to about six."

"That's six pounds saved," Noor said. "Eleven more to go."

"OK, the ribs." Remi looked at the ten internal ribs. They were solid U-shaped pieces of cardboard with triangular gussets at the base. Strong, but heavy. Eight pounds for ten ribs.

"Do I need ten ribs?" she asked.

"The books said ribs every ten inches..."

"The books were talking about WOODEN ribs in WOODEN boats. Wooden ribs are thin. My cardboard ribs are already much thicker than a wooden rib. Thicker means stronger per rib, which means I might not need as many."

She pulled out two ribs from the middle of the boat, leaving eight, spaced about twelve inches apart instead of ten.

She pressed on the hull. It flexed slightly in the gap where the ribs had been removed, but the bottom was stiff enough on its own to hold the shape.

Then the cat walked across the hull bottom and it flexed a lot more.

"Hmm," Remi said.

"The cat weighs nine pounds," Lucas said, because of course he knew that.

"I weigh sixty-two," Remi said. "If the hull flexes under a nine-pound cat, eight ribs might not be enough."

She put one rib back in, for nine total. The hull barely flexed when the cat walked across it again. The cat sat down in the middle of the boat, curled into a ball, and started purring, apparently claiming the Narwhal as her personal bed.

"Nine ribs," she decided. "That saves about three-quarters of a pound. Not much, but everything counts."

"Six and three-quarters more pounds," Noor said.

"Now the hard part. The bottom."

Remi stared at the triple-layer hull bottom. Nineteen pounds. Almost half the boat's weight. She NEEDED it to be lighter. But she also needed it to be waterproof.

She picked up her waterproofing test squares from the experiment earlier that week. Squares 7 and 8 had been nearly as good as Square 12, and they didn't have paint.

"What if I only paint the SEAMS?" Remi said.

"What?"

"The waxed cardboard and duct tape keep most of the water out. The only weak spots are where pieces overlap, the seams. What if instead of painting the entire bottom, I only paint the seam lines? That way I get the waterproofing where I need it most, and I save the weight of all that paint everywhere else."

Noor considered this. "That's... actually smart."

"It might save two or three pounds of paint."

"Five more after that."

Remi stared at the bottom. Then she picked up a piece of the triple-layer sandwich and really looked at it. Three layers. Each one double-wall cardboard.

"What if the bottom is only two layers?" she said.

"You said two layers might not hold up."

"Two layers of REGULAR cardboard might not. But what if I use two layers of the heaviest double-wall cardboard, the refrigerator box stuff, with the flutes perpendicular to each other? That's still four walls of cardboard total, plus the flutes between each pair. It's not as thick as three layers, but it might be stiff enough."

"Might be?"

"There's only one way to find out."

She made a test piece. Two layers of heavy double-wall, cross-laminated, taped together, wax on the outside, tape on the seams. She weighed it and compared it to a same-sized piece of the triple-layer bottom.

The two-layer piece was 35% lighter.

She put both pieces in the water bucket.

One hour later, she pulled them out. The two-layer piece was slightly damp on the inside. Not mushy, not soft, just barely damp.

"It's not perfect," Remi said. "But the race is fifteen minutes, not one hour. Let me test again."

She put a new two-layer piece in the bucket and pulled it out after fifteen minutes.

Completely dry.

"TWO LAYERS!" she shouted.

The math worked out beautifully. Switching from three layers to two would drop the hull bottom weight from 19 pounds to about 12. Combined with the lighter side panels, fewer ribs, and paint only on seams:

- Old weight: 47 pounds
- New hull bottom: 12 pounds (saved 7)
- New side panels: 6 pounds (saved 6)
- Nine ribs instead of ten: 7.2 pounds (saved 0.8)
- Less paint: 3 pounds paint instead of 4 (saved 1)
- Total new weight: approximately 32 pounds

"Thirty-two!" Noor said. "That's close to your target!"

"That's Marcus's weight range," Remi said. "But with better waterproofing."

She looked at the clock. It was 8 PM on a Thursday. The regatta was Saturday.

She had one day to rebuild the bottom, rebuild the sides, and do one more water test.

"Tomorrow after school," Remi said. "Can you be here at 3:30?"

"I'll be here at 3:15," Noor said.

"Lucas?"

Lucas had fallen asleep on a pile of cardboard scraps, still holding a strip of duct tape in one hand. He looked like a very small construction worker at the end of a long shift.

Remi draped a spare piece of cardboard over him like a blanket.

"Sleep well, support crew," she whispered.

Then she turned back to her Build Journal and started drawing the final version.

The Narwhal, Mark Three.
