# Chapter 8: Second Water Test

It took four days to rebuild the bottom of the Narwhal.

Four days of cutting, taping, painting, waiting for paint to dry, painting again, waiting AGAIN, and then painting one more time because Remi wasn't taking any chances.

The new bottom was a sandwich of three layers of waxed cardboard, each layer's flutes running in a different direction, taped together with overlapping strips on both sides. The water-facing side (the outside bottom) got three coats of Mom's leftover deck paint, a thick gray latex that dried to a rubbery, waterproof seal.

Mom had donated the deck paint cheerfully. "If it keeps our deck dry," she said, "it can keep your boat dry."

The seams were the part Remi was most proud of. Instead of sticking two pieces of cardboard next to each other (a butt joint, which was basically a crack that water loved to sneak through), she'd overlapped every piece by at least two inches, like shingles on a roof. Then she'd taped over the overlap, then painted over the tape.

Three layers of defense at every seam. Wax, tape, paint.

The Narwhal looked different now. The bottom was gray and smooth where the paint had dried, and the sides were still silver duct tape. Noor said it looked like a spaceship. Lucas said it looked like a shark. Remi thought it looked like a boat that was NOT going to sink.

On Thursday after school, six days before the regatta, they took it back to Miller's Pond.

This time, they had an audience.

Marcus was there, working on his own boat. He'd rebuilt it AGAIN. This was his fourth year entering, and his new design was narrower than before, with a sharp V-shaped bow and what looked like a pretty smart rib system inside. He'd clearly been doing his own research.

The Kozlov twins were there too, and their boat was... something.

It was enormous. Easily nine feet long and four feet wide, painted bright red and covered in stickers. The twins, Mila and Sasha, were eleven and seemed to believe that bigger was always better. Their strategy, as far as Remi could tell, was to build the boat so huge that it could afford to absorb a lot of water before sinking.

"That's not how buoyancy works," Remi muttered.

"Let them find out," Noor whispered back.

And then there were the Patel sisters.

Aanya and Priya Patel were setting up at the far end of the pond with quiet efficiency. Their boat was... good. Really good. It was about six feet long, neatly constructed, and painted a glossy white. It had a curved hull that looked almost professional. Aanya, who was twelve, was making adjustments to the interior while Priya, who was ten, recorded notes on a tablet.

They had a tablet. For their notes. Remi felt a little ping of nervousness.

"Their boat looks really nice," Noor said.

"Nice doesn't mean it floats," Remi said. But she was nervous.

She shook it off. She hadn't come here to look at other people's boats. She'd come here to test hers.

"OK," Remi said. "Let's do this."

They carried the Narwhal to the water's edge. Remi strapped on her life vest. Noor readied the stopwatch.

The pond was calm. A light breeze ruffled the surface. Somewhere, a frog croaked.

"Launch time," Noor said.

Remi slid the Narwhal into the water. It settled, flat and steady.

She climbed in.

The boat sank lower, just like last time, but the hull felt DIFFERENT under her feet. Firm. Solid. No give at all.

She picked up the paddle and took a stroke.

The Narwhal moved. She took another. It moved more. The pointed bow cut the water cleanly, pushing a small wave to each side.

"One minute," Noor called.

Remi paddled out to the fifty-foot mark, where she'd started getting nervous last time. She looked down. The hull bottom was completely dry inside. Not a drop.

She kept going.

"Two minutes."

Seventy feet. Eighty feet. The boat was moving smoothly, the ribs creaking softly with each stroke, the water making a gentle rushing sound against the hull. Remi pressed her hand against the bottom. Dry. Firm. No softness.

"Three minutes."

She was farther out than she'd ever been. The ducks had moved away from her, giving her suspicious looks. On the shore, she could see Noor with the stopwatch, Lucas waving with both arms, and Dad pretending to read his phone but actually watching her with laser intensity.

"Four minutes."

"Five minutes."

The bottom was still dry.

"SIX minutes."

STILL DRY. Remi wanted to scream with joy but she was too busy paddling.

"Seven minutes." Noor's voice was getting excited now. "Seven minutes, Remi! That's Marcus's record!"

Marcus, who was standing on the bank watching, shouted, "WHAT?"

"SEVEN MINUTES AND COUNTING!" Noor shouted.

"NO WAY!" Marcus shouted back.

Remi paddled. Eight minutes. Nine minutes. Ten minutes. The bottom of the hull was firm and dry. The paint-tape-wax sandwich was WORKING. Water slid along the hull like it was made of plastic. Not a drop getting through.

"Eleven minutes."

She was grinning so hard her face hurt. She turned the Narwhal around at the far side of the pond (the turn was slow and wide, the boat didn't want to turn quickly, but it turned) and started paddling back.

"Twelve minutes."

She was about eighty feet from shore on the return trip. She could see everyone watching. The Kozlov twins had stopped working on their boat to stare. The Patel sisters were watching too, Aanya with her arms crossed, Priya with her tablet up like she was taking notes on Remi's run.

"Thirteen minutes."

"Fourteen minutes."

Remi was fifty feet from shore. The race time limit was fifteen minutes. She was going to make it. She was going to do the full fifteen minutes without sinking!

"FOURTEEN THIRTY."

She paddled harder. Thirty feet. Twenty.

"FIFTEEN MINUTES!" Noor clicked the stopwatch and jumped up and down. "FIFTEEN MINUTES AND THE HULL IS INTACT!"

Remi paddled to shore and climbed out. She immediately flipped the Narwhal over and inspected the bottom.

It was perfect. The paint was intact. The tape seams were sealed. She pressed every square inch with her fingers, and every square inch was firm and dry.

"It survived fifteen minutes," she said.

"You did it!" Lucas was hopping around like a frog.

"I did NOT do it," Remi said.

Everyone stopped celebrating.

"What do you mean?" Noor asked.

Remi looked at her stopwatch. "Fifteen minutes. That was the time limit. But it took me fifteen minutes just to go out and come back, and I didn't even go the full distance. I turned around at maybe 150 meters. The race is 200 meters out and 200 meters back."

She did the math in her head. "At this speed, the full race distance would take me about twenty minutes. The time limit is fifteen."

Silence.

"So..." Noor said.

"So the boat floats. The waterproofing works. But it's too slow." Remi ran her hand along the hull. "It's too heavy. All that extra cardboard and tape and paint made it waterproof, but it also made it weigh a ton. I'm paddling a bathtub."

She sat down on the dock and opened her Build Journal.

"I need to make it lighter," she said. "Without making it weaker. Or less waterproof."

"Is that even possible?" Noor asked.

"It has to be possible. Otherwise nobody would ever finish the race."

Marcus walked over. He'd been listening.

"Your waterproofing is incredible," he said. "I've never seen a cardboard hull last that long. But yeah, your boat is heavy. I could tell by how low it sat in the water."

Remi nodded. "How much does yours weigh?"

"About thirty pounds."

Remi winced. "Mine's forty-seven."

"Oof."

"Yeah."

Marcus sat down next to her. "You know what's weird? My boat is light, but it leaks. Your boat is waterproof, but it's slow. If we could combine the two..."

"Then we'd have a boat that actually finishes the race."

They looked at each other. For a second Remi thought he was going to suggest they team up, and she wasn't sure how she felt about that. But Marcus just shrugged and said, "Good luck figuring it out. I'll be working on my seams."

He walked back to his boat.

Remi stared at the pond. The water was flat and green and patient. It didn't care about her boat or her waterproofing or her schedule. It was just water, doing what water does.

She needed to lose seventeen pounds from the Narwhal without losing any of its strength or waterproofing.

She needed to do it in six days.

She picked up her pencil and started drawing.
