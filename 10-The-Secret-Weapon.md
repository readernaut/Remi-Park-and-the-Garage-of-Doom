# Chapter 10: The Secret Weapon

Friday after school. One day until the regatta. Remi was in the Garage of Doom doing a final inspection of the Narwhal Mark Three.

She'd rebuilt the whole boat in a single afternoon. Noor said this was "insane." Remi said it was "efficient." Lucas said he was tired and wanted a sandwich.

The Mark Three was beautiful. It was leaner than the earlier versions, with triangular cutouts in the side panels that made it look fast even sitting still. The two-layer hull bottom was smooth and gray with painted seams. The nine internal ribs held it all together in a skeleton of triangles.

She put it on the bathroom scale. (Mom did not know about this. Mom did not need to know about this.)

Thirty-two pounds.

Close enough.

But she needed to test it on water. The Mark Three was lighter and built differently than the Mark Two, and Remi wasn't going to show up to the race without knowing if it actually worked. She'd learned that lesson from the Marshmallow Mark Five. (Never assume something is fine just because it looks fine. The Mark Five had looked GREAT right up until the fire.)

They loaded the boat into Dad's car one more time and drove to Miller's Pond.

Remi put on her life vest, slid the Mark Three into the water, and climbed in.

It felt completely different.

The boat was lighter, and she could FEEL it. Where the Mark Two had sat heavy in the water like a bathtub, the Mark Three rode higher. It bobbed when she shifted her weight. The hull bottom was firm under her feet, no softness at all, and the triangular cutouts in the sides made the boat feel open and airy instead of boxed in.

She picked up the paddle and took a stroke.

The Narwhal MOVED.

Not the sluggish crawl of the Mark Two. A real, honest surge forward. The lighter hull meant every stroke counted more. She paddled harder, and the boat picked up speed, the pointed bow slicing the water cleanly.

"How's the hull?" Noor called from the dock.

Remi pressed her hand to the bottom. "Dry. Completely dry."

She paddled out fifty feet, a hundred feet, grinning the whole way. The boat was fast. Faster than anything she'd built. The waterproofing was holding. The ribs were solid. This was WORKING.

Then she tried to turn around.

The Narwhal kept going straight. She paddled hard on the right side to turn left. The boat drifted, slowly, reluctantly, like a dog being dragged away from a squirrel. She paddled harder. The boat eventually came around, but it took almost thirty seconds and a wide, sloppy arc that would waste precious time in the race.

Turning was terrible. Straight-line speed was great, but the race had a turnaround at the far dock. A thirty-second turn could cost her everything.

She paddled back to shore, frowning.

"The good news: it's fast and waterproof," she told Noor. "The bad news: it steers like a shopping cart with a stuck wheel."

That's when she looked across the pond and saw the Patel sisters.

They were doing their own practice run, and their boat was slicing through the water like it was born there. Narrow, maybe only two feet wide, with a curved hull that rode up over ripples instead of pushing through them. Aanya paddled with smooth, powerful strokes while Priya sat in the back with a small rudder, steering.

Wait. A rudder.

"They have a RUDDER," Remi said.

"Is that allowed?" Noor checked the flyer. "The rules say the boat has to be cardboard and tape. They don't say anything about the paddle or... other stuff."

Remi watched the Patel sisters turn their boat in a tight arc. Tight. Fast. Effortless. Their boat turned like a dolphin, and Remi's turned like a refrigerator.

"I need a rudder," Remi said.

"You need a rudder," Noor agreed.

Back in the Garage of Doom, Remi stared at the Narwhal's stern (the flat back end). A rudder was basically a flat blade that stuck down into the water behind the boat. When you tilted it left, the water pushed the back of the boat right, which made the front of the boat go left. It was like a vertical paddle that you used for steering instead of speed.

"The boat is cardboard," Remi said. "But the rudder doesn't have to be. The rules say the BOAT must be cardboard. A rudder is a separate piece."

She rummaged through the STUFF THAT MIGHT BE USEFUL SOMEDAY bin. At the very bottom, under a broken clock and two rolls of copper wire, she found a piece of thin plywood about eighteen inches long and six inches wide.

Perfect.

She drilled a hole in the stern and attached the plywood rudder with a bolt, a washer, and a wing nut. The bolt let the rudder pivot left and right. She taped a short wooden dowel to the top of the rudder as a handle, so she could steer while sitting in the boat.

She tested it by sitting in the boat on the garage floor and wiggling the rudder handle. The plywood blade swung smoothly from side to side.

"I can't test this on water until tomorrow," Remi said. "I'll just have to trust the physics."

"Do you trust the physics?" Noor asked.

"Always."

But there was one more thing.

The paddle.

Remi had been using a piece of plywood nailed to a broomstick, and it worked, but it was heavy and clumsy. She watched videos of kayakers and canoeists and noticed something: real paddles had wide, thin blades and long handles. The wide blade pushed more water per stroke. The long handle gave you more leverage.

She needed a real paddle. Or at least a better fake one.

"Dad," she said, walking into the kitchen. "Can I have the broken cutting board?"

The cutting board had cracked down the middle two months ago, and Mom had been meaning to throw it out. It was a flat, thin piece of hard maple, about twelve inches by eight inches.

"What do you want it for?" Dad asked.

"Paddle blade."

"Ah." Dad opened the junk drawer and pulled out the broken cutting board. "Have at it."

Remi took the cutting board, sanded the edges smooth, and drilled two holes near the top. She slid a four-foot-long wooden dowel through the holes and secured it with duct tape and two small screws.

The result was a paddle that was light, strong, and had a blade wide enough to grab a serious amount of water with each stroke. She held it up and took a practice swing.

"That looks professional," Noor said.

"It looks like a cutting board on a stick," Lucas said.

"It IS a cutting board on a stick. But it's an ENGINEERED cutting board on a stick."

She set the paddle next to the Narwhal and stepped back to look at everything.

The boat: seven feet long, thirty-two pounds, waterproof hull, triangulated ribs, triangle-cutout sides. Built from produce boxes, refrigerator boxes, and twenty-one rolls of duct tape.

The rudder: plywood, bolted through the stern, hand-steerable from the cockpit.

The paddle: maple cutting board on a wooden dowel. Light and wide.

She'd done everything she could think of. She'd researched hull shapes and buoyancy. She'd tested twelve waterproofing methods and found the best one. She'd solved the weight problem with triangles and careful material selection. She'd added a rudder for steering and built a proper paddle for speed.

"Is it enough?" Noor asked.

Remi thought about it. The Patel sisters had a good boat and a head start on practice. Marcus had three years of experience. The Kozlov twins had... enthusiasm.

"I don't know," Remi said. "But it's everything I've got."

She looked at the flyer pinned to the pegboard. *The Golden Paddle remains unclaimed. Do YOU have what it takes?*

"One thing I know for sure," Remi said. "This boat is not going to sink."

She closed her Build Journal and set it on the workbench.

Tomorrow was the race.

She was going to need a good night's sleep.

She was absolutely not going to get one.
