# Chapter 2: The Regatta

The flyer said:

---

**THE 5TH ANNUAL MILLBROOK CARDBOARD REGATTA**

**Can YOUR boat survive Miller's Pond?**

*Date: Saturday, June 14th*
*Location: Miller's Pond, Millbrook Town Park*
*Registration: Free! Sign up at the Recreation Center*

**RULES:**
1. Boats must be constructed ENTIRELY from cardboard, duct tape, and paint. No other materials.
2. Each boat must hold at least one person.
3. Race distance: across Miller's Pond and back (approximately 400 meters).
4. All racers must wear approved life vests. (Provided.)
5. Time limit: 15 minutes.
6. Have fun! (Not optional.)

**PRIZES:**
- First place: Trophy + $50 gift card to Millbrook Hardware
- "Best Sinking" Award: For the most spectacular failure
- The Golden Paddle: For the first team in regatta history to actually finish the race

*Note: In four years of racing, no boat has ever completed the course. The Golden Paddle remains unclaimed. Do YOU have what it takes?*

---

Remi read the flyer three more times.

"The Golden Paddle," she whispered.

"Nobody's ever finished," Noor said. "Four years. Zero finishers. Every boat sinks."

"Every boat so far."

They looked at each other. Noor did the thing where she pushed her glasses up her nose, which meant she was thinking. Remi did the thing where she bounced on her toes, which meant she was thinking too but louder.

"We need to go see the pond," Remi said.

Twenty minutes later, Remi, Noor, and Lucas (who had followed them on his scooter because Lucas always followed them on his scooter) stood at the edge of Miller's Pond.

The pond was wide and flat and green, with cattails along the edges and a wooden dock on each side. One dock had a sign that said START. The other dock, way across the water, had a sign that said TURN. Between them was approximately 200 meters of open water. The full race was across and back: 400 meters total.

"It's far," Noor said.

"It's not THAT far," Remi said. Then she squinted. "OK, it's pretty far."

"The water looks cold," Lucas observed.

"You're not getting in the water, Lucas."

"I didn't say I wanted to. I said it LOOKS cold. I'm making an observation. That's science."

He wasn't wrong.

They walked around the pond, and that's when they ran into Marcus Chen.

Marcus was twelve, the oldest kid in their neighborhood, and he entered the regatta every single year. He was sitting on the dock with his feet dangling over the water, sketching in a notebook.

"Hey, Marcus," Remi said.

Marcus looked up. "Oh hey, Remi. You signing up for the regatta?"

"Yep."

"Cool. You'll love it." He paused. "Your boat's going to sink, though."

"How do you know?"

"Because everybody's boat sinks. Every year. I've entered three times. My best time before sinking was seven minutes and fourteen seconds." He said this proudly, like seven minutes of not sinking was a major achievement. Which, honestly, it kind of was.

"What went wrong?" Remi asked.

Marcus sighed. "First year, the bottom dissolved. Just turned to mush. Second year, the seams popped when I sat in it. Third year..." He stared out at the water. "Third year was the closest. The hull held, but water kept seeping in through tiny gaps in the duct tape. Slow leak. By seven minutes, I was sitting in three inches of water and the cardboard was getting soft."

"So the problem is waterproofing," Remi said.

"The problem is that cardboard and water HATE each other," Marcus said. "Cardboard is basically thick paper. Paper is made of wood fibers. Wood fibers absorb water like a sponge. You can wrap it in duct tape, but if ANY water gets in, any water AT ALL, the cardboard starts breaking down from the inside."

Remi was already writing in her Build Journal. She wrote: *CARDBOARD = WOOD FIBERS. ABSORBS WATER. MUST PREVENT ALL WATER CONTACT.* She underlined ALL three times.

"Who else is racing?" Noor asked.

"The Kozlov twins always enter. They build the biggest boat every year and it's always the first to sink. Last year they built a six-foot pirate ship and it went down in forty-five seconds. They won Best Sinking."

"Anyone good?"

Marcus shrugged. "The Patel sisters are entering this year. Aanya and Priya. They're ten and twelve. I saw them doing hull tests at the pond last week."

"Hull tests?" Remi perked up. "They're testing already?"

"Looked pretty serious. They had a clipboard and everything."

Noor and Remi exchanged a glance. A clipboard. These Patel sisters meant business.

"Thanks, Marcus," Remi said.

"Good luck. Seriously." He grinned. "You're going to need it."

On the walk home, Remi was quiet. Noor knew what that meant. It meant Remi's brain was running at full speed and her mouth hadn't caught up yet.

Lucas scooted alongside them, doing little jumps off the curb. "Remi," he said. "Can I be on your team?"

"You can be our support crew."

"What does that mean?"

"It means you hand us things and cheer."

Lucas thought about this. "Can I also bring snacks?"

"Sure."

"Then I accept."

When they got home, Remi went straight to the Garage of Doom. She ripped the flyer off the workbench and pinned it to the pegboard wall, right at eye level. Then she opened her Build Journal to a fresh double-page spread and wrote at the top in big letters:

**PROJECT NARWHAL**

Because every project needed a cool name, and narwhals were basically unicorn submarines, which was exactly the kind of energy she needed.

Below the title, she wrote:

**THE GOAL:** Cross Miller's Pond and back in under 15 minutes. Be the first boat in regatta history to finish.

**THE PROBLEM:** Cardboard dissolves in water.

**THE REAL PROBLEM:** Making cardboard NOT dissolve in water, while also making a boat that's light enough to paddle fast.

She chewed on her pencil and stared at what she'd written.

Then she added one more line:

**STEP ONE:** Figure out how boats actually work.

"Noor," she called out to the driveway, where Noor was sitting on the steps, already organizing a fresh notebook of her own. "Library tomorrow?"

"Already on my list," Noor said.
