# Chapter 3: Research and Design

The Millbrook Public Library had a whole section on boats.

Remi didn't know this because she usually went straight to the engineering and science shelves, but Noor had looked it up in the catalog on the way over, because Noor always looked things up on the way over. Being best friends with Noor was like having a search engine that also packed really good trail mix.

They spread six books across a library table. Lucas installed himself in the kids' section with a book about volcanoes. He was fine.

"OK," Remi said, flipping open the biggest book. It was called *The Science of Ships* and it had cross-section diagrams of everything from Viking longships to modern aircraft carriers. "First question: why do boats float?"

"Because they're lighter than water?" Noor said.

"That's what I thought too, but no. Look at this." Remi pointed at a picture of a massive steel cargo ship. "That ship weighs THOUSANDS of tons. Steel is way heavier than water. But it floats."

"So... how?"

"Buoyancy." Remi said the word carefully, like she was unwrapping it. "It's not about what the boat weighs. It's about how much water the boat PUSHES OUT OF THE WAY."

She grabbed a piece of scratch paper and drew a quick sketch: a box sitting in water, with arrows.

"OK so imagine you put a box in a pool. The box sinks down and pushes water aside, right? The water that got pushed away wants to come back. It pushes up against the bottom of the box. That upward push is called buoyancy."

"So the water is pushing the boat up?"

"Exactly. And a boat floats as long as the upward push from the water is equal to the weight of the boat pushing down. The trick is the SHAPE. A flat piece of steel sinks, because it doesn't push much water aside. But if you shape that same steel into a big hollow bowl, it pushes away a LOT of water, and the upward push is strong enough to keep it floating."

Remi drew a steel ball (sinks) and a steel bowl (floats).

"So our cardboard boat needs to be hollow and push a lot of water aside," Noor said.

"Right. The more water we displace, the more buoyancy we get. That means wide and hollow is better than narrow and solid."

Noor was writing everything down in her notebook, neat and organized, with bullet points. Remi's notes looked like a raccoon had gotten into an art supply store, but the information was the same.

They spent two hours at the library. By the end, Remi had filled eight pages of her Build Journal and her brain felt like it might actually catch fire, but in a good way. Here's what they'd learned:

**SHAPE MATTERS.** A flat-bottomed boat is the most stable (hardest to tip over) but also the slowest through the water. A V-shaped bottom cuts through waves better and goes faster, but tips more easily. For cardboard, flat bottom was safest.

**LONG AND NARROW BEATS SHORT AND WIDE.** A longer boat moves through water with less resistance. Think kayak versus bathtub. You want kayak energy.

**RIBS ARE THE SECRET.** Real ships have internal ribs, curved frames that run across the inside of the hull. The ribs give the hull its shape and keep it from collapsing under the pressure of the water. Without ribs, the hull is just a floppy skin.

**POINTED BOW.** The front of the boat (the bow) should come to a point or at least a narrow angle. This helps the boat cut through water instead of trying to shove it out of the way, which is slow and splashy.

**SEALED SEAMS.** This was the big one. Every place where two pieces of material meet is a seam, and every seam is a potential leak. The fewer seams, the fewer leaks. The better the seams are sealed, the longer the boat survives.

Remi stared at her notes.

"I think I can design this," she said.

"You think, or you know?"

"I know. I just said 'think' to be polite."

Noor laughed.

Back in the Garage of Doom, Remi taped a huge piece of butcher paper to the workbench and started drawing. Not little Build Journal sketches this time. A real blueprint. Full size.

The boat would be seven feet long (long enough for Remi to sit in with her legs stretched out and still have room for paddling), three feet wide at the widest point (stable but not too wide), and about fourteen inches deep (high enough to keep splashes out, low enough to be light).

Flat bottom. Pointed bow. Square stern (the back), because a pointed stern was harder to build and didn't help much.

Inside: cardboard ribs every ten inches, running side to side. The ribs would be shaped like upside-down U's, following the curve of the hull. They would hold the whole thing together like a skeleton holds together a body.

Remi drew and erased and drew and erased. Noor timed her: the whole design took three hours and eleven minutes. When Remi finally stepped back, the blueprint covered the entire workbench.

"That's a boat," Noor said.

"That's a NARWHAL," Remi corrected.

"It doesn't have a horn."

"It has a pointed bow. Close enough."

Lucas wandered in from the house, eating a popsicle. He looked at the blueprint. He tilted his head. He tilted it the other way.

"That's going to sink," he said.

"LUCAS."

"I'm just saying! It's made of cardboard!"

"Everything sinks if you do it wrong," Remi said. "The point is to do it right."

She wrote the materials list at the bottom of the blueprint:

- Cardboard (a LOT)
- Duct tape (even more)
- A ruler
- A box cutter (adult supervision required, Mom, I KNOW)
- A pencil
- Determination

She looked at the last item.

Then she crossed it out and wrote: *An unreasonable amount of determination.*

"We need cardboard," Remi said. "Good cardboard. Lots of it."

"Where do you get lots of cardboard?" Noor asked.

Remi smiled. It was the smile she got right before a project went from a drawing on paper to a thing in the world, the smile that said *this is about to get real.*

"Everywhere," she said. "You just have to know where to look."
