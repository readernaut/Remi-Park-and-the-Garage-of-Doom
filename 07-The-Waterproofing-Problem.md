# Chapter 7: The Waterproofing Problem

After pancakes (blueberry, with extra syrup, because science required fuel), Remi went back to the Garage of Doom and got to work on the hardest problem she'd ever faced.

The problem, in simple terms: water kills cardboard.

The problem, in complicated terms: even when cardboard is covered in duct tape, water finds a way in. Through microscopic gaps between tape strips. Through the edges where tape doesn't quite reach. Through condensation, where warm air inside the boat meets cold water outside and creates tiny water droplets on the cardboard surface. Death by a thousand drips.

Remi needed to make the bottom of the Narwhal completely, totally, 100% waterproof. Not 99% waterproof. Not 99.9% waterproof. One hundred percent.

She sat at her workbench and thought.

Then she did what she always did when she was stuck: she experimented.

She cut twelve small squares of cardboard, each one exactly six inches by six inches. She numbered them 1 through 12 with a marker. Then she treated each one with a different waterproofing method.

**Square 1:** Plain cardboard. No treatment. (The control group. Every good experiment needs a control group, something you DON'T change so you can compare everything else against it.)

**Square 2:** One layer of duct tape on one side.

**Square 3:** Two layers of duct tape, overlapping, on one side.

**Square 4:** Duct tape on BOTH sides, completely sealed.

**Square 5:** Duct tape on one side, with the seams pressed down hard using the back of a spoon. (Remi's theory: maybe the tape wasn't sticking tight enough.)

**Square 6:** Waxed cardboard from a produce box, no tape.

**Square 7:** Waxed cardboard with one layer of duct tape.

**Square 8:** Waxed cardboard with two layers of duct tape.

**Square 9:** Regular cardboard painted with three coats of latex paint. (Paint was allowed by the rules!)

**Square 10:** Waxed cardboard painted with three coats of latex paint.

**Square 11:** Regular cardboard, duct tape, AND three coats of paint.

**Square 12:** The full treatment. Waxed cardboard, tape on both sides pressed tight, and three coats of paint on the water-facing side.

"This is a lot of squares," Noor observed.

"This is SCIENCE," Remi said.

She got a big plastic storage tub from the house, filled it with water from the garden hose, and lined all twelve squares up on the workbench.

"Here's what we're doing," she told Noor and Lucas. "We're putting each square in the water for one hour. Every fifteen minutes, we take them out, weigh them, and see how much water they absorbed. The one that absorbs the least water wins."

"How do we weigh them?" Lucas asked.

"Kitchen scale."

"Mom's going to love that," Noor said.

"Mom doesn't need to know."

(Mom found out. Mom always found out. But she just said "hmm" and went back to her book.)

They set up the experiment like a real lab. Noor made a data table in her notebook with columns for each square and rows for each time check. Lucas was in charge of the timer. Remi was in charge of the kitchen scale, which she'd smuggled out under her shirt.

They lowered all twelve squares into the tub of water.

Then they waited.

Fifteen minutes is a long time when you're watching cardboard soak.

Remi paced. Noor organized her pencil collection. Lucas read the instructions on the duct tape packaging (three times).

*BEEP BEEP BEEP.*

"Fifteen minutes!" Lucas announced.

They pulled the squares out one by one and weighed them.

Square 1 (plain cardboard) was already falling apart. It had absorbed so much water that it weighed almost twice what it started at and was as floppy as a wet napkin.

Square 2 (one layer of tape) was better but still damp on the untaped side.

Squares 3, 4, and 5 were all holding up well, but when Remi pressed them, she could feel dampness inside the cardboard, under the tape.

Square 6 (waxed cardboard, no tape) was a surprise. It was barely damp. The wax coating was doing serious work.

Squares 7 and 8 (waxed cardboard plus tape) were even better than 6. Almost completely dry. The wax was blocking most of the water, and the tape was catching whatever got past it.

Square 9 (painted) was also good. The paint had sealed the surface, and very little water had gotten through.

Squares 10, 11, and 12 were all nearly dry.

They put everything back in the water and waited another fifteen minutes.

And another.

And another.

By the one-hour mark, the results were clear.

Noor read them out:

"Square 1: destroyed. Square 2: very wet. Square 3: wet. Square 4: slightly damp. Square 5: slightly damp. Square 6: barely damp. Square 7: dry. Square 8: dry. Square 9: slightly damp. Square 10: dry. Square 11: dry. Square 12: completely dry."

Remi stared at the data.

"Square 12 wins," she said. "Waxed cardboard plus duct tape plus paint. Triple protection."

"But Square 7 and 8 were almost as good," Noor pointed out. "Waxed cardboard plus tape. No paint needed."

"True. But the race is fifteen minutes, not one hour. Let me think about what's really happening."

Remi picked up Square 12 and Square 7 and held them side by side. Both were dry. But Square 12 had three layers of protection (wax, tape, paint), and Square 7 only had two (wax, tape).

"Here's what I think," Remi said slowly. "The wax is the first line of defense. It repels most of the water. The duct tape is the second line, it seals the seams and edges where the wax doesn't cover. And the paint is the third line, filling in any tiny gaps the tape missed."

She set the squares down and started sketching in her Build Journal.

"For the Narwhal, I think we need all three on the bottom. That's the part that's IN the water the whole time. But the sides, which only get splashed, probably only need tape. And the inside can stay bare cardboard as long as we keep it dry."

"What about the seams?" Noor asked. "That's where the water got in last time. The places where two pieces of cardboard meet."

"Right." Remi chewed her pencil. "The seams need extra treatment. What if we do overlapping tape on the outside AND paint over the tape? The paint fills in any micro-gaps between tape edges."

"Like caulking a bathtub," Noor said.

"Exactly like caulking a bathtub."

They looked at each other.

"We need to rebuild the bottom," Remi said.

"The whole thing?"

"The whole bottom. New waxed cardboard, three layers cross-laminated, taped on both sides, painted on the water side. And I want the seams overlapped, not just butted together. Like shingles on a roof. Water runs OVER a shingle, it doesn't seep through."

Noor looked at her watch. "The regatta is in eight days."

"Then we better get started."

Remi pulled the soggy Narwhal onto the workbench and carefully cut away the ruined bottom, keeping the ribs and sides intact. The skeleton of the boat was fine. It just needed new skin.

Better skin. Waterproof skin.

She grabbed a produce box from the pile and started cutting.

"Lucas," she called. "I need tape."

"Eight inches?"

"Eight inches."

The sound of the box cutter filled the Garage of Doom again: *schhhk, schhhk, schhhk.*

Behind her, Noor was already updating the project timeline on her clipboard.

Eight days. They could do this.

Probably.
