# Chapter 6: First Water Test

Getting the Narwhal to Miller's Pond required Dad's car, a lot of careful lifting, and one very stressful moment when Lucas tripped over a rock and almost fell on top of it.

"LUCAS!"

"I'M FINE. THE BOAT IS FINE. EVERYTHING IS FINE."

The boat was fine. But Remi checked every seam twice just in case.

It was Saturday morning, early, because Remi wanted to test before the other regatta teams showed up. She didn't want an audience. Not yet. Not until she knew if the Narwhal actually worked.

Miller's Pond was calm and flat. A few ducks floated near the far shore, looking unbothered by everything.

Remi and Noor carried the Narwhal down to the water's edge. Dad stood on the dock holding a life vest and trying not to look worried. He was not succeeding.

"You're wearing the life vest," Dad said.

"I know, Dad."

"The WHOLE time."

"I know, Dad."

"And if anything feels wrong, you come straight back."

"I KNOW, Dad."

She put on the life vest and buckled it tight. Then she looked at the Narwhal, sitting on the muddy bank with its silver duct-tape hull gleaming in the morning light. It looked solid. It looked ready.

It also looked like it was made of cardboard and tape, which it was, and that suddenly seemed like a much bigger problem than it had in the garage.

"Moment of truth," Noor said. She had a stopwatch ready in one hand and a clipboard in the other. Noor was going to time how long the Narwhal lasted and record everything that happened. "For science," she'd said.

"For science," Remi had agreed.

"OK," Remi said. "Let's do this."

She pushed the Narwhal into the shallow water, keeping one hand on the stern. The bottom touched the pond. Water flowed around the hull.

Nothing happened.

"It's touching water," Noor reported. "No leaks visible."

Remi pushed it farther out, until it was floating in about a foot of water. She let go.

The Narwhal floated.

Not tipping. Not leaning. Not sinking. Just... floating. Steady and flat, like it was supposed to be there.

"IT FLOATS!" Lucas screamed.

"It floats," Remi whispered. Then louder: "IT FLOATS!"

Dad let out a breath he'd apparently been holding since they left the house.

"OK," Noor said, clicking the stopwatch. "Timer started. Get in."

This was the scary part.

Remi waded into the cold water, her sneakers squelching in the mud (she'd been told to wear old shoes and she now understood why), and reached the Narwhal. She gripped both sides. She took a breath.

She climbed in.

The Narwhal sank lower. The water line rose from about two inches on the outside to about six inches. But it held. The ribs creaked slightly, a soft sound of cardboard flexing under load, but they held.

Remi sat down in the middle of the boat. Water lapped at the sides.

She was floating. In a cardboard boat. On a real pond.

"THIS IS AMAZING!" she shouted.

"Time: thirty seconds," Noor called. "How does it feel?"

"Solid! The bottom feels firm. The sides are steady. The ribs are doing their job!"

She picked up her paddle (a piece of plywood nailed to a broom handle, not great, but functional) and took a stroke.

The Narwhal moved.

She took another stroke. It moved more. She was actually paddling a cardboard boat across a real body of water and it was WORKING.

She paddled out about fifty feet from shore. The ducks watched her with suspicious eyes.

"One minute," Noor called.

Remi paddled farther. The Narwhal glided along, not fast, but steady. The pointed bow cut through the tiny ripples. She could hear the soft sound of water sliding along the cardboard hull.

"Two minutes."

This was working. This was actually working. Remi started to grin.

"Three minutes."

She stopped paddling and sat still, just floating, feeling the gentle bob of the boat on the water. She looked down at the hull bottom between her feet.

That's when she noticed it.

The bottom was flexing.

Not a lot. Just a little. A soft, spongy give under her sneakers where before it had been stiff.

"Noor," she called out, keeping her voice calm. "The bottom is getting soft."

Noor looked at her stopwatch. "Three minutes, twenty seconds."

Remi pressed her hand against the hull bottom. It was warm and slightly damp. Not wet, not leaking, but the cardboard was absorbing moisture THROUGH the duct tape. Not from a leak. Just from the tiny amount of pond water that seeped through microscopic gaps between tape strips, and from the condensation forming on the underside of the hull.

"It's absorbing water," Remi called. "The cardboard is getting wet from the INSIDE."

She started paddling back. Not panicking, but moving with purpose. The bottom was getting softer under her with every second.

"Four minutes."

She was about thirty feet from shore. She could see Noor tracking her with the stopwatch, and Dad standing in the shallows ready to grab her if anything went wrong, and Lucas standing on the dock with both hands over his mouth.

"Four minutes, thirty seconds."

The bottom sagged. Not catastrophically, but enough that a small pool of water formed around her left foot where the cardboard dipped. The water was cold.

"Coming in!" Remi paddled harder.

"Five minutes."

She was almost there. Ten feet. Five feet. The bottom was definitely soft now, and the pool of water was getting bigger, and the cardboard was making a sound she didn't like, a wet, giving, crinkly sound, like a cereal box left out in the rain.

She reached the shallows and scrambled out of the boat. Dad grabbed one side, she grabbed the other, and they dragged the Narwhal onto the bank.

They all looked at it.

The top half of the boat looked fine. The sides were firm, the ribs were solid, the bow was still sharp and pointed.

The bottom was a disaster.

The triple-layer hull had absorbed so much water that the middle layer was mushy. Remi poked it with her finger and it went right through, like poking wet bread. The duct tape was still intact on the surface, but underneath it, the cardboard had turned into something that was more sponge than ship.

"Five minutes and eight seconds," Noor said, clicking the stopwatch. "Not bad."

"Not ENOUGH," Remi said. "The race is fifteen minutes."

She stood there, looking at her soggy, sagging, slowly-collapsing boat, with pond water dripping off her sneakers and a piece of duct tape stuck to her elbow, and she felt... disappointed? Sad?

No.

She felt determined.

Because the boat had floated for five minutes. Five minutes! In four years of the regatta, Marcus's record was seven minutes. She'd hit five on her FIRST TRY.

The design worked. The structure worked. The shape worked.

The waterproofing didn't work.

And that meant there was only one problem to solve.

"Noor," Remi said. "We need to figure out waterproofing."

"I know." Noor held up her clipboard. She'd written a whole page of observations during the test. "I have some ideas."

"Me too."

They looked at each other and grinned. It was the grin of two people who had just watched their boat almost dissolve, and instead of being discouraged, were already thinking about how to make the next version better.

"Can we get pancakes first?" Lucas asked.

"Yes," said Remi. "We can absolutely get pancakes first."
