# Chapter 4: The Materials Run

The Materials Run happened on a Wednesday after school, and it was the most fun Remi had ever had in the back of her dad's car.

The plan was simple: drive around Millbrook and collect as much free cardboard as possible. The execution was chaos.

**Stop 1: Greenway Grocery.**

Remi marched up to the produce manager, a tall man with a mustache named Doug, and said, "Excuse me, do you have any large cardboard boxes I could have?"

Doug looked at her. He looked at Noor, who was holding a clipboard. He looked at Lucas, who was holding a juice box.

"What's it for?" Doug asked.

"I'm building a boat."

"Out of cardboard?"

"Yes."

Doug stared at her for a long moment. Then he said, "Follow me."

He took them to the back of the grocery store, through a door marked EMPLOYEES ONLY (Remi tried not to look too excited about going through a door marked EMPLOYEES ONLY, but she was VERY excited), and into a storage room that was wall-to-wall cardboard boxes.

"Take whatever you want," Doug said. "You'd be doing me a favor. We have to break all these down for recycling anyway."

Remi's eyes went wide.

The boxes were beautiful. Not regular flimsy shipping boxes, but thick, heavy-duty produce boxes with wax coatings on the inside. This was the good stuff.

"Noor," Remi said. "NOOR."

"I see it."

"The wax coating. These boxes are designed to hold wet produce. Fruits and vegetables. They're already partially waterproof."

Noor was writing furiously on her clipboard. "Waxed cardboard from produce boxes. Source: Greenway Grocery. Free."

They loaded fourteen boxes into Dad's car. Lucas sat in the back with boxes stacked on both sides of him like cardboard walls.

"I feel like I'm in a fort," Lucas said.

"You ARE in a fort," Remi said.

**Stop 2: Henderson's Appliances.**

This was the big-ticket stop. Mr. Henderson (their neighbor, the marshmallow-in-coffee victim) owned an appliance store downtown. When Remi had asked him the day before if he had any large boxes, he'd said, "Kid, I've got boxes that a refrigerator came in. How big do you need?"

Very big, it turned out.

Mr. Henderson had saved three refrigerator boxes, two washing-machine boxes, and one box that had held a commercial pizza oven, which was so big Remi could stand inside it with her arms stretched wide and not touch the sides.

"This is amazing," Remi breathed.

"These are double-walled," Mr. Henderson said, rapping his knuckles on the side of a fridge box. "See? Two layers of cardboard with a wavy layer in between. That wavy layer is called the flute. It's what gives cardboard its strength."

"How do you know that?" Noor asked.

"I've been selling things that come in boxes for thirty-five years. You learn about boxes." He winked. "Also, I did the cardboard regatta in 1987."

"YOU did the regatta?!" Remi nearly dropped her Build Journal.

"The first one. Before it was official. Just a bunch of guys at the firehouse who bet each other they could cross the pond in a cardboard box. I lasted about ninety seconds." He chuckled. "Used single-wall cardboard. No tape at all. Just folded the flaps under and hoped for the best."

"That's... not a great strategy."

"I was twenty-two and very confident. Two things that don't always go together."

He also handed them a case of duct tape, brand-new, still in the plastic. "Bought this at Costco during the panic-buying days. Never used it. Take it."

They loaded everything into Dad's car, which was now so full of cardboard that Dad had to peer through a tiny gap between boxes to see out the rearview mirror.

"This is fine," Dad said, in a voice that meant it was not entirely fine.

"One more stop!" Remi said.

**Stop 3: The Recycling Center.**

The Millbrook Recycling Center was a gold mine.

Huge bins of flattened cardboard, sorted by type. Remi walked through them like a kid in a candy store, except instead of candy she was looking at corrugation patterns and wall thickness.

"This one," she said, pulling out a sheet. "Double-wall, C-flute. Feel how stiff it is."

Noor felt it. "It barely bends."

"Perfect for the hull. And THIS one," she grabbed another sheet, thinner, "single-wall, B-flute. Lighter but still strong. Good for the ribs and internal structure."

"How do you know all the flute types?" Noor asked.

"I looked them up last night. There are five main types: A, B, C, E, and F. A-flute is the thickest and cushiest, good for protecting stuff in shipping but too spongy for a boat. C-flute is the best balance of stiff and strong. B-flute is thinner but still structural. E and F are basically fancy paper."

Noor wrote it all down.

They filled the remaining space in Dad's car with recycling-center cardboard. Dad could no longer see out of ANY mirror.

"We're done," Remi announced. "Home, please."

"Can I see the road?" Dad asked.

"Mostly."

"Mostly," Dad repeated. "Great."

Back at the Garage of Doom, they unloaded everything and sorted it into piles:

**PILE 1: HULL MATERIAL.** Double-wall waxed produce boxes and C-flute sheets. The strongest, most water-resistant stuff. This would form the outer skin of the boat.

**PILE 2: STRUCTURAL MATERIAL.** Double-wall fridge and washer boxes. This would be cut into ribs and the internal skeleton.

**PILE 3: RIB MATERIAL.** Lighter B-flute cardboard. Easier to cut and shape into the curved rib pieces.

**PILE 4: MISCELLANEOUS.** Odd pieces that might be useful for patches, reinforcements, or things Remi hadn't thought of yet. She dumped this pile into the STUFF THAT MIGHT BE USEFUL SOMEDAY bin, which was now overflowing onto the floor.

The duct tape went on the workbench: twenty-four rolls, silver and shining, stacked like a tiny fortress.

"That's a lot of tape," Lucas said.

"That's not a lot of tape," Remi said. "That's BARELY enough tape."

She stood in the middle of the Garage of Doom, surrounded by mountains of cardboard, looking at her blueprint on the workbench, and felt that feeling she loved more than almost anything, the feeling that sat right between excitement and terror.

She was about to build something that might actually work.

Or it might sink in forty-five seconds like the Kozlov twins' pirate ship.

But either way, she was going to build it.

"Noor," she said. "Can you come back tomorrow after school?"

"Already on my calendar."

"Lucas?"

"Yes?"

"I'm going to need you to cut tape."

Lucas stood up very straight, like a soldier receiving orders. "I can do that."

"I need pieces exactly eight inches long. Can you measure that?"

"I can measure ANYTHING." He pulled a tape measure out of his pocket. He'd been carrying it around for three days.

Remi looked at her team. Her best friend with the clipboard. Her little brother with the tape measure. Twenty-four rolls of duct tape and enough cardboard to build a house.

"OK," she said. "Let's build a boat."
