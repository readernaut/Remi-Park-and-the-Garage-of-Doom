# Chapter 11: Regatta Day

Remi barely slept.

She lay in bed staring at the ceiling, thinking about seams and buoyancy and the Patel sisters' rudder and whether thirty-two pounds was light enough and whether the paint would hold and whether the ribs would creak or crack and whether she'd remembered to tape the inside corner where the bow met the hull bottom because she couldn't remember if she'd taped that corner and now it was 2 AM and she STILL couldn't remember.

At 2:17 AM she got out of bed, went to the Garage of Doom in her pajamas, and checked.

She'd taped it. Obviously she'd taped it. She'd been thorough. She was ALWAYS thorough.

She went back to bed.

At 2:23 AM she got up again and checked the OTHER inside corner.

Also taped.

She went back to bed and finally fell asleep at some point, because the next thing she knew, Lucas was standing over her saying, "REMI IT'S RACE DAY WAKE UP WAKE UP WAKE UP."

The Millbrook Cardboard Regatta started at 10 AM. At 8:30 AM, the Park family loaded the Narwhal Mark Three into Dad's car (again) and drove to Miller's Pond.

The pond was barely recognizable.

Red, white, and blue bunting hung from the dock posts. A banner stretched between two trees: THE 5TH ANNUAL MILLBROOK CARDBOARD REGATTA. There was a registration table staffed by two volunteers from the recreation center. There was a hot dog cart. There were at least a hundred people.

A hundred people.

"Noor," Remi whispered, suddenly unable to feel her hands. "There are a lot of people here."

"Yep," Noor said, adjusting her clipboard.

"I thought this was a small town thing."

"It's Millbrook's most popular event. I told you this."

"You did NOT."

"I definitely did. You were probably thinking about hull shapes."

That was fair.

They registered at the table and got their race number: 4. Remi wrote it on the side of the Narwhal with a marker. Then they carried the boat down to the launch area, where the other teams were assembling.

There were six boats total.

**Boat 1: The Kozlov twins' "Red Menace."** It was eight feet long and painted fire-engine red with a skull and crossbones on the bow. It was the biggest boat by far. The twins were wearing matching red bandanas and seemed extremely excited.

**Boat 2: Marcus Chen's "Ghost."** Sleek, light gray, narrow. Marcus had clearly done some redesigning since Remi last saw it. It looked faster. He was making last-minute adjustments with a roll of duct tape.

**Boat 3: The Patel sisters' "Stiletto."** Remi's stomach clenched. The Stiletto was gorgeous. Long, narrow, polished. It sat in the water like it belonged there. Aanya and Priya were stretching, like actual athletes before a race.

**Boat 4: The Narwhal Mark Three.**

**Boat 5: A boat called "The Cardboard Titanic,"** built by two kids Remi didn't know, a boy and girl who looked about ten. Their boat was wide and flat and had the words NOT UNSINKABLE painted on the side, which Remi thought was either very funny or very concerning.

**Boat 6: A boat that looked like a bathtub.** Literally a rectangle with no bow, no stern, and no discernible strategy. The builder was a kid named Owen who was nine and had apparently started building it yesterday.

"Owen," Noor whispered. "His boat is a BOX."

"Everyone starts somewhere," Remi said. But she couldn't help smiling.

At 9:45, the race marshal, a large man with a megaphone named Ron, gathered all the teams at the start dock.

"Welcome to the Fifth Annual Millbrook Cardboard Regatta!" Ron's megaphone crackled. "Here are the rules. Your boats must be in the water by 10 AM. At the starting horn, you'll race to the TURN dock across the pond, go around the buoy, and race back to the START dock. Total distance approximately 400 meters. Time limit: fifteen minutes. If your boat sinks, please swim to shore. Life vests are mandatory. Are there any questions?"

Owen raised his hand. "What if my boat sinks immediately?"

"Then you'll be part of a proud tradition," Ron said. "Any other questions? No? Excellent. Boats in the water!"

The next ten minutes were chaos.

The Kozlov twins launched the Red Menace with a running push, yelling a war cry. It hit the water with a splash and bobbed wildly. They scrambled in, nearly tipping it, and settled with water sloshing over the sides.

Marcus slid the Ghost in smoothly and climbed in with practiced ease. He sat very still, checking the waterline.

The Patel sisters launched the Stiletto with military precision. Aanya got in first, then Priya. The boat barely dipped. They picked up their paddles and sat motionless, waiting.

Owen shoved his box into the water. It floated, but it spun slowly in a circle. Owen climbed in and sat in the middle like a kid in a very wet sandbox.

The Cardboard Titanic team launched their boat and immediately started arguing about who should sit in front.

Remi waded into the water, holding the Narwhal steady. She climbed in. The boat sank to its mark, about six inches below the top edge, and held firm. She grabbed the rudder handle with her left hand and the paddle with her right.

This was it.

She looked at Noor, standing on the dock with the stopwatch.

Noor gave her a thumbs-up.

She looked at Lucas, standing next to Dad, wearing his tape measure on his belt like a tool.

Lucas waved with both hands.

She looked at the water stretching out in front of her, all the way to the turn dock, 200 meters away. It looked very far. It looked like a different country.

The horn blew.

SIX BOATS SURGED FORWARD.

The Red Menace took off with a huge splash as both Kozlov twins paddled furiously on the same side, which made the boat veer hard to the left. "OTHER SIDE!" Mila screamed. "PADDLE THE OTHER SIDE!" They corrected, zigzagging across the water like a drunk shopping cart.

Marcus's Ghost shot ahead, low and fast. He paddled with a smooth rhythm, his boat cutting the water cleanly.

The Stiletto moved like a knife. Aanya paddled in the front, Priya steered from the back, and the boat barely seemed to touch the water. It just glided.

Owen's box spun in a slow circle at the starting line. Owen paddled on one side, the box turned. He paddled on the other side, the box turned the other way. He was going nowhere.

The Cardboard Titanic was still arguing.

And Remi?

Remi paddled.

The first stroke felt good. The cutting-board blade bit into the water and PULLED. The Narwhal surged forward. The second stroke, the third, the fourth. Each one stronger as Remi found her rhythm.

Stroke. Stroke. Stroke.

She was moving. Not as fast as the Stiletto. Not as fast as the Ghost. But moving, steadily and strongly, the pointed bow cutting the water, the rudder keeping her straight.

"ONE MINUTE!" she heard Noor yell from shore.

She checked her position. The Stiletto was about thirty feet ahead. Marcus was twenty feet ahead. The Red Menace was somewhere to her left, still zigzagging. Everyone else was behind her.

She kept paddling.

At the two-minute mark, the first boat sank.

It was the Cardboard Titanic. They'd stopped arguing and started paddling, but the wide, flat hull had been scooping water over the front with every stroke. By two minutes, the floor was an inch deep in pond water, and the cardboard was giving up. The two kids scrambled out as their boat slowly, majestically folded in on itself and slipped beneath the surface.

The crowd on shore cheered. First sinking of the day.

"TWO DOWN, FOUR TO GO," Ron announced over the megaphone.

At three minutes, Owen's box sank. Owen hadn't actually gone anywhere, he'd just been spinning near the start dock, so the sinking happened about ten feet from shore. He climbed out, stood waist-deep in water, and said, "I'll do better next year."

"That's the spirit!" Ron said.

At four minutes, the Red Menace sank.

This was spectacular.

The Kozlov twins had actually gotten halfway across the pond, which was their best performance ever. But the Red Menace had been taking on water from the start (the sides were too low), and by four minutes, Mila was sitting in six inches of water. The cardboard ribs gave way with a sound like someone stepping on a bag of chips, and the whole boat split in half. The two halves drifted apart with one Kozlov twin on each piece, floating away from each other in slow motion.

"MILA!" Sasha yelled.

"SASHA!" Mila yelled.

They both rolled into the water, laughing, and swam to shore.

"Best Sinking award: CLAIMED," Ron announced. The crowd went wild.

Three boats left. Marcus. The Patel sisters. Remi.

Five minutes. Remi was past the halfway point. The turn dock was getting closer. Her arms were burning. Paddling a boat for five minutes straight was EXHAUSTING. She switched the paddle to her left hand for a few strokes, giving her right arm a break.

The rudder was working. Every time the boat drifted off course, she adjusted the handle and the Narwhal corrected. It wasn't as smooth as the Stiletto's steering, but it worked.

Six minutes. Marcus was approaching the turn dock. The Patels were right behind him.

At six and a half minutes, Marcus's boat started leaking.

Remi saw it from fifty feet back. A dark spot spreading on the side of the Ghost, just above the waterline. The seam where the side panel met the hull bottom was giving way. Water trickled in, then flowed.

"No, no, no," she heard Marcus mutter. He paddled faster, trying to reach the turn dock before his boat flooded.

He made it. Barely. He rounded the buoy with water sloshing around his feet, and his boat was riding two inches lower than before.

The Patels rounded the buoy five seconds later, smooth and clean. No leaks. No problems.

Remi reached the turn dock at seven minutes. She grabbed the buoy, swung the Narwhal around (the rudder made this SO much easier than it would have been without it), and started the return trip.

The bottom of her boat was dry. Completely dry.

Seven minutes and thirty seconds. Marcus's boat was in trouble. He was paddling hard on the return leg, but the Ghost was sinking. The leak had spread. His paddle strokes were getting desperate.

At eight minutes, Marcus sank.

It was slow and sad. The Ghost didn't break apart like the Red Menace. It just... settled. Lower and lower, like a deflating balloon, until Marcus was sitting in a cardboard puddle at water level, his paddle resting on the surface of the pond.

"Well," Marcus said. He slid out of the remains of his boat and floated on his back in his life vest. "Eight minutes, twelve seconds. New personal best."

From the shore, the crowd cheered for him. He raised a fist in salute.

Two boats. The Stiletto and the Narwhal.

Nine minutes.

The Patel sisters were about forty feet ahead of Remi. Their boat was still moving fast, still slicing the water, still looking absolutely perfect.

Remi paddled harder.

Ten minutes. The shore was getting closer. She could see Noor, both fists clenched, stopwatch forgotten. She could see Lucas, jumping up and down. She could see Dad, not even pretending to be calm anymore.

Eleven minutes. The Stiletto was thirty feet from the finish. Remi was sixty feet back. She wasn't going to catch them. They were too fast.

But she didn't need to catch them.

She just needed to finish.

Eleven minutes and thirty seconds. The Stiletto crossed the finish line.

The crowd ERUPTED. Aanya and Priya raised their paddles over their heads. They'd done it. The first team to ever finish the Millbrook Cardboard Regatta.

Remi barely heard it. She was paddling. Stroke, stroke, stroke. The Narwhal was solid beneath her. The hull was dry. The ribs were holding. Everything she'd built, everything she'd tested and fixed and rebuilt and tested again, was working exactly like it was supposed to.

Twelve minutes.

Forty feet to go.

Stroke. Stroke. Stroke.

Thirty feet.

Her arms were on fire. Her shoulders screamed. Sweat dripped off her nose.

Twenty feet.

She could see the dock. She could see the finish line marker. She could see Noor, who was now screaming something Remi couldn't hear over the crowd.

Ten feet.

Five.

The bow of the Narwhal bumped the dock.

"TWELVE MINUTES AND FORTY-SEVEN SECONDS!" Ron's megaphone crackled. "WE HAVE A SECOND FINISHER! BOAT NUMBER FOUR, THE NARWHAL!"

The crowd exploded again. Remi sat in her boat, paddle across her lap, breathing so hard she thought her lungs might quit, and felt something huge and warm bloom in her chest.

She'd finished.

She hadn't won. The Patel sisters had been faster. But she'd FINISHED. She'd crossed Miller's Pond and come back. In a boat she'd built from cardboard and duct tape in a garage that smelled like paint and rubber bands.

Noor ran down the dock, skidded to a stop at the edge, and pulled Remi into a hug that almost tipped them both into the water.

"YOU DID IT!" Noor screamed.

"WE did it," Remi said. "I built it. You made sure it didn't fall apart."

Lucas arrived at the dock, out of breath, tape measure swinging from his belt. He looked at Remi. He looked at the boat. His eyes were enormous.

"Can I sit in it?" he whispered.

Remi climbed out and held the Narwhal steady against the dock. Lucas climbed in very carefully, sat down cross-legged in the middle, and looked around.

"I'm on a boat," he said.

"You're on a boat."

"I'm on a boat made of CARDBOARD."

"You are."

Lucas broke into the biggest grin Remi had ever seen on his face. "This is the best day of my life," he announced.

Remi believed him. It might have been the best day of hers, too.
